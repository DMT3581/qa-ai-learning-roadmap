## In one line

A model only knows what it learned in training plus what you put in front of it right now — context engineering is deciding what information it actually sees, giving it everything it needs to succeed and nothing that distracts.

## Why this matters for QA

Most "the AI gave me a useless, generic, or wrong answer" moments are not phrasing problems — they are context problems. The model does not know your application, your test framework's conventions, the requirement behind a ticket, or the actual error you are seeing, unless you put those things in the conversation. Give the same model the relevant spec, the real (sanitized) error, your conventions, and a clear output format, and the quality of what comes back changes dramatically. Learning to supply the right context — no more, no less — is often a bigger lever than any clever wording.

## Learning objectives

After this chapter you will be able to:

- Explain what "context" is and why the model is blind to anything outside it.
- Avoid the two failure modes: too little context and too much.
- Choose what information to include, and structure it so the model can use it.
- Manage context over longer interactions and respect confidentiality while doing so.

## Core content

### Prompt engineering vs. context engineering

Prompt engineering is about how you phrase the instruction. Context engineering is the broader discipline of curating the whole set of information the model sees when it answers — your instructions, the task input, reference material, examples, relevant history, and the output format. Anthropic describes it as the natural progression of prompt engineering: as tasks get larger, the question shifts from "what words do I use?" to "what configuration of information is most likely to produce the result I want?"

### The model is blind to everything outside the context

A model has two sources of knowledge: what it absorbed during training, and what is in the conversation right now. It cannot see your files, your repository, your screen, your situation, or a chat you had last week — unless that information is present in the current context. The simple rule: if it isn't in the window, it doesn't exist for the model. Whatever it needs and couldn't possibly know, you have to supply.

### Two failure modes

- **Too little context (under-supplying).** With gaps, the model fills them by guessing or generalizing — which is where generic answers and hallucinations come from.
- **Too much context (over-supplying).** Dumping everything seems safe but backfires. The context window is finite, and research shows a "context rot" effect: as the number of tokens grows, the model's ability to reliably use any one of them decreases. Key information gets buried in the noise. More is not better.

The goal is the smallest set of high-signal information that lets the model succeed — maximum signal, minimum noise.

### What to include

For everyday work, useful context usually means some combination of:

- **Clear instructions** — what you want done (see Prompt Anatomy).
- **The actual input** — the text, code, requirement, or error you're working on.
- **Reference material** — the spec, the doc, the relevant code, the real (sanitized) log. Grounding the answer in a source you provide both improves accuracy and makes it easy to verify.
- **Examples** — a sample of what "good" looks like (few-shot), especially for format or style.
- **Only the relevant history** — what matters for this task, not everything you've ever discussed.
- **The output format** — table, list, code in a specific style, length.

### Curate, don't dump

Paste the relevant slice, not the whole thing. The model rarely needs your entire repository to fix one function — it needs that function, the interface it calls, and the conventions to follow. Cutting the irrelevant parts improves the answer and reduces both confidentiality exposure and context rot.

### Structure it

Label the parts so the model can tell instructions from data: headings, delimiters, or tags like `## Requirement`, `## Code`, `## Output format`. A structured context is easier for the model to use correctly — and easier for you to maintain and reuse.

### Manage the budget over longer interactions

The window is finite (see Context Windows), and very long contexts tend to bury important details in the middle. Put the things that matter where they'll be noticed, and for long-running work, periodically summarize and restart with a clean, compacted context rather than letting a thread balloon (the restart move from Prompt vs. Conversation). This discipline matters even more with agents and long tasks — but the habit starts in everyday chats.

### Confidentiality gate

"The right information" must also be information you are allowed to share. Before pasting context — code, logs, data — apply the rules from Confidentiality and Sensitive Data: sanitize identifiers, strip secrets, use approved tools. Good context engineering and good data hygiene are the same motion done well.

## Examples

**Weak (under-context).** "Write tests for my login function." → The model invents an interface and produces generic happy-path tests, because it knows nothing about your actual function.

**Strong (curated context).**
```
## Requirement
Users lock out after 5 failed attempts for 15 minutes.

## Function (sanitized)
async function login(email: string, password: string): Promise<AuthResult>

## Conventions
Playwright + TypeScript; follow this existing test as a pattern: [short example].

## Task
Write tests covering valid login, invalid credentials, lockout after 5 attempts,
and lockout expiry. Output as Playwright test code.
```
Same model, vastly more useful output — because it now has exactly what it needs and little else.

**Over-context (also weak).** Pasting the entire repository and twenty log files "so it has everything" buries the relevant function, risks leaking data, and degrades the answer. Give the relevant module and the specific failing log lines instead.

## Common pitfalls

- Assuming the model knows your context. It doesn't — supply it.
- Dumping everything. Whole repos and full logs add noise, risk, and context rot.
- Burying the key instruction in a wall of text where it gets lost.
- Letting a long thread accumulate stale context. Summarize and restart.
- Forgetting to sanitize what you put into context.

## Key takeaways

- The model only knows its training plus the current context — supply what it can't know.
- Both too little and too much context hurt; aim for the smallest high-signal set.
- Curate and structure the information; ground answers in sources you provide.
- Manage the budget over long interactions, and sanitize before you share.
