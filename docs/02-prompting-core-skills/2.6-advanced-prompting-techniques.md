## In one line

Beyond basic prompting there are a few named techniques worth knowing — role, few-shot examples, structured instructions, and step-by-step reasoning — each suited to a specific job, not a universal "make it smarter" switch.

## Why this matters for QA

Once you've got the basics, these techniques are how you get consistent, well-formatted, reliable output — exactly what you want when generating test cases in a fixed format or doing repeatable analysis. The catch is that this area is full of folklore, especially the "you are a world-class expert" persona prompt, and the evidence says some of it doesn't do what people believe. Knowing which technique actually helps with what saves you from copying prompts that don't move the needle — and points you at the ones that do.

## Core content

### Role / persona prompting — for tone and perspective, not magic accuracy

Role prompting means assigning the model a persona: "You are a senior QA engineer…". It's the most popular advanced technique, and also the most misunderstood.

The honest evidence: on modern models, role prompting does not reliably improve accuracy on reasoning or factual tasks. Studies that tested many personas across thousands of questions found no dependable gain, and step-by-step reasoning consistently beat persona prompts on reasoning problems. A persona mainly helps when your base prompt is weak; on an already-clear prompt it adds little.

Where role prompting genuinely helps is open-ended and communication tasks — controlling tone, style, perspective, and audience. Tell it to write as a travel writer and it will; tell it to explain a defect for a non-technical manager and the framing shifts usefully. It's also handy for setting a reviewing perspective ("review this as a skeptical senior QA," from AI as a Peer Reviewer).

So: use a role to steer voice and viewpoint, not as a smartness boost. "You are an expert" won't fix accuracy — grounding and verification do that. When you need reasoning, reach for examples or step-by-step instead.

### Few-shot — show, don't just tell (the most reliable lever)

Few-shot means including a few worked examples of input → output in the prompt (zero-shot is none; few-shot is a handful). Across tasks it's the most consistent technique, and it's the strongest lever for getting a specific format or style — the model imitates the pattern you show it.

Doing it well:

- Use 2–5 high-quality, representative examples.
- Cover the variety you expect, including an edge case.
- Keep examples consistent in format — inconsistent examples confuse the model.
- Watch for bias: skewed examples skew output. If every example you show is a "pass" case, the model will lean toward passing.

For QA this is gold: show one test case in your exact table format and ask for ten more like it, and you get your format, not the model's.

### Structured instructions — make output reliable and parseable

Organize the prompt itself: separate instructions from data with clear sections, delimiters, or tags, and specify the output shape. This builds on Writing Clear Constraints and Context Engineering. Use headings like `## Task`, `## Input`, `## Output format`, and request a concrete schema (named table columns, JSON). Structure improves reliability, makes the output easy to reuse or parse, and makes the prompt easier for you to maintain and reuse.

### Chain-of-thought — for genuine reasoning

Asking the model to work step by step before answering improves multi-step, arithmetic, and logic tasks, and few-shot combined with step-by-step reasoning is often the best setup for reasoning-heavy work. Two caveats: it's task-dependent (it can even hurt very simple tasks), and modern "reasoning" models already do this internally, so adding it explicitly matters less with them. It's still useful when you want to see the reasoning so you can check it.

### Match the technique to the task

| You need… | Reach for… |
|---|---|
| A tone, audience, or perspective | Role |
| A specific format or consistency | Few-shot examples |
| Reliable, parseable output | Structured instructions |
| Multi-step reasoning | Step-by-step (with few-shot) |
| Accuracy on facts | None of these — grounding + verification (Context Engineering, Fact-Checking) |

And they combine. Layer a role for tone, examples for format, and structure for parseability — then iterate (Prompt Iteration).

## Examples

**Few-shot for format.** "Here is one test case in our format: [example row]. Generate ten more for this feature in exactly this format." → output matches your table.

**Role for communication.** "Explain this defect to a non-technical product manager, in three short sentences." → appropriate tone and level.

**Anti-pattern.** "You are a world-class expert tester with 30 years of experience" — expecting markedly better test cases. The effect is minimal; you'll get far more from giving an example, naming the test-design technique, and supplying context.

### Try it yourself (optional)

Take a prompt you already use and add exactly one technique — a single worked example, say. Run both versions and compare. Doing this in isolation shows you which technique actually moves your output, instead of stacking four and guessing.

## Common pitfalls

- Cargo-culting "you are an expert." It rarely improves accuracy on modern models.
- Biased few-shot examples. Unrepresentative examples skew the output.
- Stacking every technique on a trivial task. Match effort to the task.
- Expecting techniques to fix accuracy. Only grounding and verification do that.
- Inconsistent example formats. They confuse rather than guide.

## Key takeaways

- Role steers tone and perspective; it's not a reliable accuracy boost on modern models.
- Few-shot examples are the most consistent lever, especially for format and style.
- Structured instructions make output reliable and reusable.
- Match the technique to the task, combine them, and verify facts separately.
