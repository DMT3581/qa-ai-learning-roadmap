# Cost vs Value: When AI Is Worth Using

## In one line

AI is worth using when its value clearly beats its full cost — and the cost is far more than the subscription: it's the time to prompt, verify, and fix, plus quality risk and the skills you don't want to lose.

### Why this matters for QA

You now have the habit and a way to measure it; this is the decision that follows — deciding, per task, whether AI is actually worth reaching for. The trap is treating "it's available" or "it's free" as a reason to use it everywhere. The real question is whether the value beats the *full* cost, and much of that cost is hidden: the verification and rework time, the cognitive load of overseeing output, and the risk of deskilling on things you should keep sharp.

## Core content

### The full cost ledger

Money is the smallest part. The real costs of using AI on a task:

- **Loop time** — prompting + verifying + fixing. If that exceeds doing it yourself, the value is negative (the "productivity tax").
- **Verification and oversight** — a real and growing category of work. Validating AI output can *add* effort rather than remove it; surveys have found many knowledge workers say AI made their workload heavier, not lighter, and heavy-oversight use raises cognitive load and error rates.
- **Quality risk** — the cost of a wrong output that slips through, which climbs with the stakes.
- **Skill atrophy** — leaning on AI for things you should keep sharp costs you later (see *AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment*).
- **Confidentiality / compliance risk** — using the wrong tool for sensitive data (see *Confidentiality and Sensitive Data*).
- **Subscription / credits** — the visible bit, and usually the least of it.

### The value side

Net time saved across the whole loop, quality lift, drudgery removed, capability unlocked (breadth, a first draft when you're stuck, tackling something outside your comfort zone), and learning. Value only counts if it's real and measured, not vendor-promised (see *Measuring Personal Productivity Gains*).

### The rule: value has to *clearly* beat full cost

Use AI when net value clearly exceeds the full cost; skip it when the loop is longer, quality suffers, the stakes are high with hard-to-catch errors, or you'd deskill on something core. "Clearly" is the operative word — a marginal, uncertain gain usually isn't worth the overhead and risk.

### Frequency × per-use gain

A small gain on a task you do twenty times a day is big value — worth building a workflow and a habit around. A big gain on a once-a-year task may not be worth the setup. Prioritize the frequent, repetitive tasks.

### Where AI is usually worth it — and usually not

- **Usually worth it:** repetitive work, first drafts, breadth, format transformation, beating the blank page, unfamiliar territory.
- **Usually not:** tiny one-offs where prompting overhead beats just doing it; tasks you're already expert and fast at; high-stakes work where verification cost swamps the gain; genuine novel reasoning or anything needing your private context.

### Don't let "it's there" decide

"It's free," "it's open," "everyone's using it" are not reasons — cost versus value is (see *How to Choose the Right AI Tool*). And reassess over time: value rises as models improve and as you build prompts and skill, so re-measure rather than deciding once forever.

### As an ambassador

Help colleagues put AI on the tasks where it clearly pays and take it off the ones where it doesn't. Stacking small, proven wins builds trust and results faster than spraying AI across everything.

## Examples

- **Generate test cases from a story** (frequent, repetitive, breadth) → clearly worth it.
- **Fix a one-line typo in a test** → prompting overhead beats doing it → not worth it.
- **Review a critical release doc** (high stakes) → the AI first pass is valuable *if* you verify → worth it, with care.
- **Decide a defect's severity** → judgment call → keep it human; low AI value.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

List your five most frequent tasks. For each, jot the rough full cost (prompt + verify + fix) against the value with AI. Keep AI on the clear wins, drop it from the clear losses, and stop agonizing over the ambiguous middle — those aren't where the value is anyway.

## Common pitfalls

- **Counting only the subscription.** The loop and oversight time cost more.
- **Using AI because it's available.** Availability isn't value.
- **Ignoring deskilling and confidentiality costs.** They're real, just delayed or hidden.
- **Forcing AI onto tiny one-offs.** Overhead beats the task.
- **Chasing marginal, unmeasured gains.** "Clearly beats cost" is the bar.
- **Deciding once, forever.** Re-assess as tools and your skills improve.

## Key takeaways

- The cost of AI is mostly hidden: prompt + verify + fix time, oversight load, quality and skill risk.
- Use AI when value clearly beats the full cost; skip it otherwise.
- Prioritize frequent, repetitive tasks; keep judgment-heavy and tiny one-offs manual.
- Re-assess over time, and base the call on cost-value, not availability or hype.
