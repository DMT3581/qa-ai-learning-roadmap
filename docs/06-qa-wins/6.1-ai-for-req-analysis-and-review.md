# AI for Requirements Analysis and Review

## In one line

AI can scan a requirement or user story in seconds for ambiguity, contradictions, gaps, and untestable statements — sharpening your review and the tests that follow — as long as you treat it as an advisor, not the authority.

### Why this matters for QA

Reviewing requirements for clarity and testability is core QA work, and it's shift-left at its most valuable: a defect caught in a requirement is far cheaper to fix than the same defect caught in test or production. But requirements review is tedious and easy to skim, so issues slip through. AI gives you a fast, consistent first pass — it's good at flagging vague terms ("fast", "user-friendly"), missing acceptance criteria, contradictions, and statements you couldn't write a test against. And it pays off twice: better requirements in means better test cases out, which is exactly what feeds *AI for Test Design and Test Case Generation*.

## Core content

### What AI can spot in requirements

- **Ambiguity and vague terms** — "fast", "secure", "intuitive", "should" — words you can't write a test against.
- **Missing or weak acceptance criteria.**
- **Contradictions and inconsistencies** — within a requirement or across several.
- **Completeness gaps** — unhandled cases, error paths, and non-functional requirements (performance, security) that were never stated.
- **Untestable statements** — anything with no measurable expected result.
- **Implicit assumptions** that should be made explicit.

It can also go further and *propose* rewrites, draft acceptance criteria, and generate the clarifying questions a tester would ask — drafts for you to confirm, not answers to accept.

### Drive it with a quality framework

As with naming a test-design technique, naming the lens sharply improves the review:

- **INVEST** for user stories — Independent, Negotiable, Valuable, Estimable, Small, **Testable**. The Testable lens is the QA sweet spot.
- **Testability check** — "For each statement, can I write a pass/fail test? If not, flag it and say why."
- **Ambiguity hunt** — "List every vague or unmeasurable term and suggest a precise, testable alternative."
- **Three-amigos questions** — "Generate the clarifying questions a tester should ask the product owner before this is ready."

### Give it the right context

Apply *Context Engineering*: provide the requirement, any related requirements (so it can spot contradictions), the domain constraints it can't infer, and your acceptance-criteria format or definition of done. And sanitize first — requirements often contain client and product detail (see *Confidentiality and Sensitive Data*).

### The dual-gate reality

Research on using LLMs for requirements quality points to a "dual-gate" approach: deterministic, rule-based checks for structural hygiene, plus the LLM as an **advisory** reviewer for the subtle, contextual issues — with explicit human oversight. LLMs are strong at catching contextual anomalies and producing actionable rewrites that resemble human review, but they also generate off-topic notes, stylistic opinions, and false positives. So a human validates the findings. And the sycophancy caveat from *AI as a Peer Reviewer* applies in full: ask for problems and gaps, never "is this requirement good?"

### Turn findings into action

The output of an AI requirements review is not a verdict — it's a worklist:

- **Clarifying questions** to take to the product owner or business analyst. Don't let AI (or yourself) resolve ambiguity by guessing; surface it.
- **Draft acceptance criteria** for the team to confirm.
- **A prioritized list of issues** to resolve before test design begins.

### Caveats

- It can't know your **business context**, so it will miss domain-specific gaps and may flag non-issues.
- It may "improve" a requirement into something subtly **different** — confirm the intent is preserved.
- Resolving ambiguity is a **stakeholder decision**, not something to delegate to the model (see *AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment*).

## Examples

**Weak.** "Is this user story okay?" → "Yes, it's clear and well-formed." (Sycophancy; you learned nothing.)

**Strong.** "Act as a QA reviewer. For this user story, list as a table: (1) ambiguous or unmeasurable terms, (2) missing acceptance criteria, (3) any statement I couldn't write a pass/fail test for, and (4) clarifying questions for the product owner. Do not list strengths. Story: *As a user, the system should load my feed quickly and show relevant articles.*"

→ Flags "quickly" (unmeasurable — what's the target?) and "relevant" (undefined — by what rule?), notes there are no acceptance criteria, and asks the questions you'd take to the PO.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Take a real or sample user story and run the strong prompt above. Then write down which flagged issues are genuine, which are noise, and the top three clarifying questions you'd actually raise. The judgment about which findings matter is the QA skill — the AI just widened the net.

## Common pitfalls

- **Asking "is this good?"** Ask for problems and gaps instead.
- **Resolving ambiguity by guessing.** Surface it as a question; don't let the model decide product behaviour.
- **Trusting flagged issues blindly.** Expect false positives and off-topic noise; validate.
- **Accepting AI rewrites unchecked.** Confirm the original intent is preserved.
- **Pasting sensitive requirements into unapproved tools.** Sanitize and use approved tools.

## Key takeaways

- AI is a fast first pass for ambiguity, contradictions, gaps, and untestability.
- Name the lens — INVEST or a testability check — to focus the review.
- Output is a worklist of clarifying questions, not a verdict.
- Keep it advisory: a human validates, and stakeholders resolve ambiguity.
