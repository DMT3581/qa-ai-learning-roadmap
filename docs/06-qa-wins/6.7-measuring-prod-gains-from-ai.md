# Measuring Personal Productivity Gains from AI

## In one line

People routinely feel more productive with AI than they actually are — a rigorous study found experienced developers were 19% *slower* while believing they were faster — so measure honestly instead of trusting the feeling.

### Why this matters for QA

You'll be asked, and asked to help colleagues answer, "is AI actually helping?" — and the honest answer needs evidence, not vibes. Measuring is native QA thinking: you don't accept "it feels faster," you test it. This matters more than usual here, because the feeling of productivity is genuinely unreliable. As an ambassador, honest measurement is also exactly what the central team wants from you: honest insights, not polished success stories.

## Core content

### The feeling of productivity is unreliable

The clearest evidence comes from a 2025 randomized controlled trial by METR. It took experienced open-source developers working on their *own* large, familiar codebases and randomly allowed or disallowed AI on real tasks. The result surprised everyone: developers were about **19% slower** with AI — yet they had predicted a 24% speedup and, even *after* finishing, still estimated AI had made them about 20% faster. The gap between felt and measured productivity was so large that the deepest finding wasn't the slowdown itself, but that the developers couldn't tell.

Why the illusion? AI often makes work feel *easier* — less blank-page friction, less staring — and easier is not the same as faster. The lesson isn't "AI always slows you down" (that study was experienced people on mature code; beginners, greenfield work, and boilerplate often genuinely gain). The lesson is that **your perception isn't a measurement**, so you have to actually check.

### Measure the whole loop, not the generation

The classic mistake is "AI wrote it in 10 seconds!" while ignoring the 15 minutes of prompting, reviewing, and fixing. Real productivity is the total time from task-start to an *acceptable* result, compared to your baseline without AI — including the verification tax (see *Evaluating AI Outputs*) and any rework. Instant generation means nothing if the loop around it is longer.

### Decide what "productivity" means — it isn't only speed

Track the things that actually matter:

- **Time** — end-to-end, not just the generation.
- **Quality** — did it meet the bar, or need heavy rework? Were errors caught later?
- **Rework / rejection rate** — a useful signal: if you're rewriting or rejecting more than half of what AI gives you on a task, it's probably slowing you down.
- **Effort** — drudgery removed versus new work added.

Speed bought with worse quality, or with a pile of verification, is not a gain.

### Run a simple, honest comparison

You don't need a controlled trial. Practical options:

- **Before/after:** time the same kind of task with and without AI, a few times each — not once, because there's variance.
- **A/B on real work:** do some tasks with AI, some without, and compare time *and* quality.
- **A light log:** task, with/without AI, rough time, a quality/rework note. A handful of honest data points beats a strong feeling.

Always track quality alongside time, or you'll optimize for speed and quietly ship worse work.

### Gains are task-specific — measure per task type

Study after study finds gains depend on the task and context: substantial on drafting, boilerplate, transformation, and unfamiliar territory; negative on complex or novel work, or on things you're already expert and fast at. Don't generalize one number across your whole job. Measure per task type, keep AI where it wins, and drop it where it doesn't (which leads into *Cost vs Value*).

### As an ambassador: honest data beats success theater

Your value to the central team is honest insight — where AI helped, where it didn't, and where colleagues struggle. Measured reality builds more credibility and better decisions than a handful of cherry-picked wins.

## Examples

- "AI writes my test cases faster" → time the *whole* loop (generate + review + prune), compare to doing it manually, and check the cases were actually good. The feeling of speed isn't the measurement.
- A prompt that feels great but where you rewrite half the output → high rework, small real gain.
- Log one week: which tasks AI genuinely sped up, and which it quietly slowed.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Pick one recurring task. Do it about three times with AI and three times without over a week, logging total time and a quick quality note each time. Let the numbers — not the feeling — decide whether AI stays on that task. You'll likely be surprised at least once.

## Common pitfalls

- **Trusting the feeling of speed.** Perception and reality can diverge sharply.
- **Counting only the generation.** Measure task-start to acceptable result.
- **Ignoring quality and rework.** Fast-but-wrong isn't productive.
- **Cherry-picking wins.** Remember the times it wasted your time too.
- **The novelty effect.** A new tool feels great for a while; re-measure later.
- **Over-generalizing.** One task's gain doesn't transfer to all tasks.

## Key takeaways

- Perceived productivity is unreliable — measured developers were slower while feeling faster.
- Measure the whole loop and quality, not just how fast the output appeared.
- A few honest before/after data points beat a strong impression.
- Gains are task-specific; keep AI where it measurably wins.
