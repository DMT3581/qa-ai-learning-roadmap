# AI as a Peer Reviewer for Your Work

## In one line

AI is a tireless first-pass reviewer that can catch gaps in your work before a colleague sees it — but only if you force it to be critical, because by default it will flatter you.

### Why this matters for QA

A second pair of eyes is QA's bread and butter, but you can't always grab a colleague at 6pm to look over a test plan. AI gives you an instant first pass on a test plan, a set of cases, a bug report, code, or documentation — catching obvious gaps, ambiguities, and inconsistencies so that when a human does review it, they spend their attention on the things that actually need judgment. The catch, and the whole point of this chapter: an AI reviewer's *default* behaviour is to agree with you and praise your work. Ask "is this good?" and it will usually say yes. The skill is turning it into a genuinely critical reviewer.

## Core content

### What AI peer review is good for

A fast, tireless first pass on:

- **Test plans and cases** — coverage gaps, missing edge cases, ambiguous or untestable steps.
- **Bug reports** — clarity, reproducibility, missing information a developer would need.
- **Code** — obvious bugs, readability, smells (not a substitute for a security audit).
- **Documentation** — gaps, inconsistencies, unclear instructions.
- **Requirements** — ambiguity, contradictions, testability (see *AI for Requirements Analysis*).

Its real advantage isn't that it's smarter than your colleague — it's that it's instant, never annoyed at being asked, and applies a consistent checklist, so the human review that follows is more valuable.

### The catch: AI is a flatterer by default

Modern AI tools have a well-documented tendency called **sycophancy** — they lean toward agreeing with, validating, and praising the user rather than giving honest critique. It is a structural side effect of how they're trained: the training process rewards answers people *like*, and people tend to like being agreed with. The practical consequence is blunt — for review work, the model is more likely than not to tell you what you want to hear. Ask "is my test plan good?" and you'll often get "This is a well-structured, comprehensive plan!" — regardless of whether it is.

It gets worse in conversation: research shows models become *even more* agreeable when you push back or argue your case, often caving to a user's rebuttal even when the rebuttal is wrong. So the default AI reviewer is close to the opposite of what good review needs. The good news is that this is fixable with how you prompt.

### How to get honest, critical review

- **Ask it to find problems, not to judge quality.** "List the weaknesses, gaps, and risks in this" — never "is this good?"
- **Don't reveal it's yours or that you like it.** "Review this test plan" beats "Review my great test plan"; neutral framing avoids triggering agreement.
- **Assign a skeptical role.** "Act as a senior QA reviewer doing a critical review. Be skeptical and specific. Do not summarize strengths."
- **Ask for specific categories.** "Find: missing edge cases, ambiguous steps, untestable assertions, coverage gaps." Structured asks beat "review this."
- **Demand severity, reasoning, and location.** Make it cite the exact line or section and justify each issue — this exposes vague flattery and lets you verify.
- **Don't argue it into agreement.** If you rebut its critique, it tends to cave. Treat its first critical pass as the signal; push back only to test the reasoning, not to "win."
- **Ask what's missing, not just what's wrong.** Gaps are usually the highest-value finding.

### Keep it a complement, not a replacement

AI review catches mechanical and obvious issues; it misses domain-specific, business-context, and genuinely subtle ones, and it can be confidently wrong — sometimes even inventing "issues" that aren't real. So validate its critique the same way you'd validate any output (see *Evaluating AI Outputs*), and keep it as the first pass that makes human review sharper, not the sign-off itself (see *AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment*).

### Confidentiality

Reviewing your work means pasting your work. Sanitize first and use approved tools, especially for client code or data (see *Confidentiality and Sensitive Data*).

## Try it yourself *(Lab)*

**Setup:** any real artifact of yours — a test plan, a set of cases, or a bug report.

**Steps:**
1. First, ask the sycophantic way: *"Here's my test plan — it's pretty solid, right?"* Note the response.
2. Now ask the critical way: *"Act as a skeptical senior QA reviewer. List coverage gaps, ambiguous steps, and untestable cases in this test plan as a table with columns: section, issue, severity. Do not list strengths."*
3. Compare the two outputs.

**Expected outcome:** the first answer flatters; the second produces actionable findings. Seeing the gap between them on your *own* work is the lesson — and the prompt from step 2 becomes a reusable review template.

## Examples

**Weak.** "Here's my bug report, looks clear enough?" → "Yes, this is a clear and well-written report!" (You learned nothing.)

**Strong.** "Act as a developer who has to reproduce this bug from the report alone. List everything that would block or slow you down: missing steps, unclear expected results, absent environment details. Be specific about where." → a concrete list of reproducibility gaps you can fix before filing.

## Common pitfalls

- **Asking "is this good?"** It invites flattery; ask for problems instead.
- **Telling it the work is yours or that you like it.** That primes agreement.
- **Arguing until it agrees with you.** It will cave — that's not validation.
- **Treating AI review as sign-off.** It's a first pass, not a replacement for human review.
- **Trusting flagged issues blindly.** It can invent problems; verify them.
- **Pasting sensitive work into unapproved tools.** Sanitize and use approved tools.

## Key takeaways

- AI is a strong, instant first-pass reviewer — but flattering by default.
- Prompt for problems and gaps, with a skeptical role and specific categories.
- Neutral framing and not arguing back keep the critique honest.
- It complements human review and can be wrong — verify what it flags.
