## In one line

An AI model learns in stages — first it soaks up patterns from a huge pile of text (pretraining), then it's shaped into a helpful, safe assistant (fine-tuning) — and crucially, as a user you don't retrain it; you steer it at use-time through prompting and context.

## Why this matters for QA

You don't need to build models, but knowing roughly how they're made explains a lot of their behaviour — where their knowledge (and their cutoff, and their bias) come from, why they have a "personality" and quirks, and, most usefully, why *you* influence them through prompting and context rather than retraining. It also kills a common misconception: the model doesn't learn from your corrections during a chat. Understanding the pipeline turns "the AI just does weird things" into "ah, that comes from this stage."

## Learning objectives

After this chapter you will be able to:

- Describe the main stages of how a model is trained.
- Explain what each stage contributes to the model's behaviour.
- Distinguish training (done by the maker) from adaptation (what you do at use-time).

## Core content

### Stage 1 — Pretraining: soaking up patterns

The model (a transformer) is exposed to a vast amount of text and trained to predict the next token, over and over (see *What Is a Large Language Model*). No rules are written; it learns language, world knowledge, and reasoning-like patterns purely from the data. This is the expensive stage, done once, and it produces a **base model** — fluent and knowledgeable, but "raw": it just *continues* text and doesn't yet follow instructions. Three things trace back to here: what the model knows, its **knowledge cutoff** (see *Practical Limitations*), and any **bias** in the data (see *Bias in AI*).

### Stage 2 — Fine-tuning: becoming a helpful assistant

The base predictor is then refined on a much smaller, curated dataset to shape its behaviour. Two parts matter:

- **Instruction tuning (SFT).** Trained on examples of good assistant behaviour — answering clearly, following formatting, declining harmful requests politely. This turns "continue the text" into "follow my instruction." Important: fine-tuning shapes *behaviour and style, not facts* — it doesn't teach the model new knowledge.
- **Preference alignment (RLHF).** Humans compare responses and mark which they prefer; the model is steered toward the preferred ones — more helpful, honest, and harmless. This is where the assistant's "personality" and its guardrails come from. It's also where some quirks originate: the tendency to be over-agreeable (see *AI as a Peer Reviewer*) and to guess confidently rather than say "I don't know" (see *Hallucinations*).

Makers can also do **domain fine-tuning** — adapting a model on specialized data for a field like legal or medical work.

### Stage 3 — Adaptation: steering it without retraining

Here's the part that matters most for you. Training and fine-tuning are done by the model's maker and permanently change the model. But you can change its *behaviour per use* without touching it at all:

- **Prompting and examples (in-context learning).** Give it instructions and a few examples in the prompt, and it adapts for that session (see *Context Engineering* and *Advanced Prompting Techniques*).
- **Grounding / retrieval.** Supply external or current information at query time (see *Fact-Checking AI Responses* and *Context Engineering*).
- **Tools.** Extend what it can do — search, run code.

This is why prompt and context engineering is your real lever: you're not retraining the model, you're adapting it at use-time.

### A myth to retire: it doesn't learn from your chat

The model does not learn from your conversation or your corrections — in-context "learning" lasts only for the current session and changes nothing permanently (see *Context Windows and Memory Limitations*). Any "memory" feature is the app re-supplying context, not the model having learned.

## Examples

- **"Why doesn't it know our latest framework?"** → knowledge cutoff, from pretraining.
- **"Why does it always agree with me?"** → preference alignment (RLHF).
- **"Can I train it on our test conventions?"** → as a user you *adapt* it via context, instructions, and examples; only the maker fine-tunes.
- **"It got that wrong — will it remember next time?"** → no; in-context learning is temporary.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Correct the model on something within a chat, then start a fresh chat and ask the same thing again. It won't remember the correction. That's the training-versus-adaptation line, felt directly — and the reason your steering has to happen in the prompt and context, every time.

## Common pitfalls

- **Thinking fine-tuning adds facts.** It shapes behaviour, not knowledge.
- **Believing the model learns from your conversations.** It doesn't, by default.
- **Expecting to "train" it as a user.** You adapt via context; you don't retrain.
- **Forgetting knowledge, cutoff, and bias all trace back to the training data.**

## Key takeaways

- Pretraining gives the model its knowledge (and its cutoff and biases) from raw text.
- Fine-tuning (SFT + RLHF) turns the raw predictor into a helpful, safe assistant — and creates some quirks.
- Fine-tuning shapes behaviour, not facts; the model doesn't learn from your chats.
- As a user you adapt the model at use-time through prompting, context, grounding, and tools.
