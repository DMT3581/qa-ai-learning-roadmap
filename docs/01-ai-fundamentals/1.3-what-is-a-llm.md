## In one line

An LLM is a very large neural network trained on enormous amounts of text that works, at its core, by predicting the next piece of text one step at a time — sophisticated autocomplete at a scale that produces fluent language.

## Why this matters for QA

Almost every AI tool you'll use day to day is an LLM, and one idea explains most of their behaviour: they predict the next token. Once you internalize "it's a next-word predictor, not a knower," the rest stops being mysterious — why it's fluent yet sometimes confidently wrong, why the same prompt gives different answers, why context matters so much, and why it's brilliant at drafting but needs fact-checking. This chapter is the mental model that sits under all the practical ones.

## Learning objectives

After this chapter you will be able to:

- Explain what an LLM is in plain terms.
- Describe how it generates text through next-token prediction.
- Connect the "predict the next token" idea to its strengths and its failures.

## Core content

### What an LLM is

An LLM is a category of deep-learning model — a very large neural network — trained on immense amounts of text. "Large" refers to both the training data (much of the web and more) and the model's size (billions of parameters). It's built on an architecture called the **transformer**, which is good at handling sequences of words and capturing patterns across them. At its core, an LLM is a giant statistical prediction machine that repeatedly predicts the next piece of text.

### How it learns, in one paragraph

Nobody writes rules into it. It's trained by a simple game played an astronomical number of times: given some text, predict the next token; check against the real next token; adjust; repeat. Because the "answer" is just the next piece of the text itself, it needs no manual labels — it learns from raw text. From this single objective, at scale, it absorbs grammar, facts, writing styles, code patterns, and reasoning-like structures. (More on training in *How AI Models Learn*.)

### How it generates text

When you send a prompt, the model breaks it into **tokens** — word pieces (see *Tokens Explained*) — and then generates its response **one token at a time**: at each step it calculates probabilities for all possible next tokens, picks a likely one, feeds it back in, and repeats until it's finished. It doesn't know the whole answer in advance; it's making a best guess at every step. In effect, it's your phone's autocomplete, scaled up enormously.

### It's a predictor, not a knower

This is the mental model to keep. An LLM produces text that *statistically fits* — not text it has checked as true. That one fact explains a great deal:

- **Fluent but sometimes wrong** — the very mechanism that writes beautifully can also produce false things with total confidence (see *Hallucinations*).
- **Variable** — it chooses among likely tokens, so the same prompt can give different answers (see *Why AI Is Probabilistic*).
- **Context-dependent** — it can only "see" a limited window of text at once and predicts based on what's in it (see *Context Windows and Memory Limitations*).
- **Strong at language, weaker outside it** — excellent at drafting and transformation, shaky at exact math and current facts (see *Practical Limitations of AI*).

### From raw predictor to helpful assistant

A raw next-token predictor would just *continue* text, not follow your instructions. A further training step — fine-tuning, including learning from human feedback — shapes it into the helpful, instruction-following assistant you actually chat with. This is also where some quirks come from, like a tendency to be over-agreeable (see *AI as a Peer Reviewer*).

## Examples

- **"The capital of France is ___"** → it predicts "Paris" because that overwhelmingly follows in its training text — not because it "looked it up."
- **Ask it for a citation** and it may predict a plausible-looking reference that doesn't exist — the same "predict what fits" mechanism, now working against you (see *Fact-Checking AI Responses*).

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Type the start of a very common phrase and watch the model complete it confidently. Then ask for an obscure, specific fact and notice it sounds just as confident — even where it might be wrong. Same mechanism, very different reliability. That contrast *is* the lesson.

## Common pitfalls

- **Thinking the LLM "knows" or "looks up" facts.** It predicts what fits.
- **Reading fluency or confidence as correctness.** The tone is not evidence.
- **Expecting identical answers every time.** It's probabilistic.
- **Forgetting it only sees the current context.** No context, no knowledge of it.

## Key takeaways

- An LLM is a large neural network that generates text by predicting the next token, one at a time.
- It learns from raw text with no manual labels; scale is what gives it its abilities.
- It's a predictor, not a knower — which explains fluency-with-errors, variability, and context-dependence.
- Fine-tuning turns the raw predictor into the instruction-following assistant you use.
