## In one line

A hallucination is when AI states something plausible-sounding but false with complete confidence — not a rare glitch, but a structural consequence of a system that predicts likely text and is trained to guess rather than admit "I don't know."

## Why this matters for QA

Hallucination is the single biggest reason you verify AI output. It isn't an occasional bug that'll be patched away — it's built into how these models work, which means a fabricated fact, citation, or API can appear in fluent, confident prose at any time. For QA, a hallucinated detail that reaches a deliverable is a defect *you* introduced. Knowing why hallucinations happen — and where they're most likely — tells you exactly where to check hardest.

## Learning objectives

After this chapter you will be able to:

- Define what an AI hallucination is.
- Explain why hallucinations happen — and why they can't be fully removed.
- Recognize where they're most likely, and how to reduce and catch them.

## Core content

### What a hallucination is

A hallucination is content that sounds plausible but is false, fabricated, or unsupported — stated with the same confidence as a correct answer. It isn't lying (there's no intent) and it isn't a random glitch; it's the model generating a fluent continuation that simply happens not to be true.

### Why they happen

Two layers, both rooted in the earlier fundamentals:

- **It predicts plausible text, not truth.** As a next-token predictor (see *What Is a Large Language Model*), the model produces what statistically fits — which usually, but not always, lines up with the truth. It has no built-in fact-checker, and where its knowledge is thin, it fills the gap with something plausible-looking.
- **It's trained to guess rather than say "I don't know."** A landmark 2025 finding (OpenAI's *Why Language Models Hallucinate*) is that standard training and grading reward guessing over admitting uncertainty — like a multiple-choice exam where a guess might score points and "I don't know" guarantees zero. Models optimized to look good on such tests learn to answer confidently rather than hedge. In that sense, a confident wrong answer is exactly what the incentives encouraged.

Add ambiguous prompts (it guesses instead of asking) and a knowledge cutoff (it invents current facts), and you have the main sources.

### It's structural, not a bug to be patched

Because it's inherent to how LLMs work, hallucination can be reduced but not eliminated. Even the best current models still do it — more so on niche topics, non-English languages, and multimodal tasks. Retrieval and web search help, but don't fully solve it (the model can still misread a real source).

### Where it's most likely

Specific facts, dates, statistics, and names; **citations and sources** (see *Fact-Checking AI Responses*); made-up code functions or APIs; anything niche or recent; and answers to ambiguous questions. These are your check-hardest zones.

### How to reduce and catch it

- **Ground it** — give the model the source and ask it to answer from that (see *Context Engineering*).
- **Allow uncertainty** — tell it to say "I don't know" or flag assumptions rather than guess.
- **Use tools or search** for current or factual questions.
- **Verify** — the confident tone is not evidence (see *Evaluating AI Outputs* and *Common AI Failure Modes*).

## Examples

- **AI cites a testing standard or a Playwright API that doesn't exist** → a hallucination; verify against the real docs.
- **You ask an ambiguous question** → it confidently answers a version you didn't mean; clarify instead of accepting the guess.
- **A fluent, authoritative paragraph with one wrong figure** → it looks right and isn't; check the number.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Ask for something obscure and specific in your domain — a niche config value, a rarely-used API — then verify it. Notice the confidence is identical whether the answer is right or invented. That indistinguishable confidence is the whole reason verification isn't optional.

## Common pitfalls

- **Treating hallucination as a rare glitch** rather than a structural trait.
- **Reading confidence or fluency as correctness.**
- **Trusting specifics** — facts, citations, APIs — without checking.
- **Asking ambiguous questions** and accepting the guess.
- **Assuming a newer model or RAG makes it safe.** Both reduce, neither eliminates.

## Key takeaways

- Hallucinations are plausible but false outputs, delivered as confidently as correct ones.
- They come from predicting likely text plus training that rewards guessing over "I don't know."
- They're structural — reducible but not eliminable, even in frontier models.
- Ground, allow uncertainty, use tools, and verify — especially specifics, citations, and code.
