## In one line

"AI" is the whole field; an LLM is one specific kind of AI — the language-specialized type behind ChatGPT and Claude — so every LLM is AI, but most AI isn't an LLM.

## Why this matters for QA

In everyday talk, "AI" and "ChatGPT" have become almost synonymous, and that quietly narrows how people think. Knowing that LLMs are just one branch of AI helps you two ways: you pick the right *kind* of tool (an LLM isn't always the answer — sometimes a rule, a formula, or a purpose-built model fits better and is more reliable), and you understand *why* an LLM behaves the way it does (it's a language predictor, not a general problem-solver). It also underpins every tool-choice decision later in the program.

## Core content

### Nested categories

The cleanest way to see it is as nested boxes:

**AI ⊃ Machine Learning ⊃ Deep Learning ⊃ LLMs.**

- **AI** — the broad goal: systems doing tasks we associate with intelligence.
- **Machine learning** — systems that learn patterns from data rather than being explicitly programmed.
- **Deep learning** — machine learning that uses neural networks.
- **LLM** — a large neural network trained on huge amounts of text to predict language.

(A related branch, *generative AI*, is the content-creating kind of AI; LLMs are its text-generating members.) The takeaway: every LLM is AI, but most AI is not an LLM.

### AI that isn't an LLM

Plenty of familiar AI has nothing to do with language models: recommendation engines (streaming, shopping), computer vision (face unlock, image search), speech recognition, fraud detection and forecasting (predictive ML), rule-based and expert systems, robotics control, and game AI. Most of these are **narrow AI** — very good at one specific thing.

### What an LLM is, briefly

An LLM is a model trained to predict the next piece of text, producing fluent language; it's what powers chatbots and coding assistants. It's strong at language tasks and weak at things outside that lane — exact math, real-time data — precisely because of *what kind* of AI it is (see *Practical Limitations of AI*, and *What Is a Large Language Model* for the mechanics).

### Even the best LLMs are still "narrow AI"

Today's leading models are impressive, but they're still narrow: they excel at language, not at arbitrary human tasks. The general form that could reason across everything — AGI — is still hypothetical. An LLM is a specialized tool, not a mind.

### Why the distinction matters

- **Tool choice.** An LLM isn't always right. A validation check is just code (not AI at all); flaky-test analysis may be better as a statistical/ML model than a chatbot; forecasting is predictive ML. Reaching for an LLM by reflex can be slower, costlier, or less reliable than the right fit (see *How to Choose the Right AI Tool*).
- **Expectations.** Knowing it's a language predictor explains both its strengths and its failures — don't expect a calculator or a database.
- **Reading the ecosystem.** When a product "adds AI," ask *what kind* — an LLM chatbot, a predictive model, or a rules engine? That changes how you'd reason about and test it.

## Examples

- **"Our product now has AI"** → is it an LLM feature, a predictive model, or rules? Each is tested differently.
- **Detecting flaky tests** → likely better as statistical/ML analysis than asking a chatbot.
- **Validating an email format** → just a regex or rule — not AI at all.
- **Drafting, summarizing, explaining** → that's squarely the LLM's lane.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

List three "AI" things you use this week. For each, ask: is this an LLM, or another kind of AI? You'll probably find at least one that isn't an LLM at all — which is the whole point.

## Common pitfalls

- **Using "AI" and "LLM/ChatGPT" as synonyms.** They're a field and one branch of it.
- **Reaching for an LLM** when a rule or purpose-built model fits better.
- **Expecting an LLM to do** what other AI — or plain code — does better.
- **Assuming every "AI feature" is an LLM.**

## Key takeaways

- AI is the broad field; LLMs are one specialized, language-focused kind of AI.
- Much everyday AI (vision, recommendations, forecasting, rules) isn't an LLM.
- Even the best LLMs are narrow AI — specialized tools, not general minds.
- Knowing the difference sharpens both tool choice and expectations.
