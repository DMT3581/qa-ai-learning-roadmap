## In one line

Today's AI has real, predictable blind spots — exact math, current and private information, and genuine step-by-step reasoning — and knowing them tells you what to hand off, what to keep, and where to expect it to fail.

## Why this matters for QA

Knowing a tool's limits is just risk awareness pointed at your own toolkit. It tells you which tasks are safe to delegate, which to keep, and — usefully for QA — where to expect failure so you can watch for it. Asking AI to do something it is structurally bad at (count your test cases exactly, recall the latest framework API, predict how your private system behaves) sets you up to be confidently misled. The point isn't to distrust AI; it's to use it where it's strong and stop asking it to be something it isn't.

## Learning objectives

After this chapter you will be able to:

- Name the main practical limitations of current AI tools.
- Predict where the model is likely to fail before you rely on it.
- Decide what to delegate, what to keep, and what to offload to a deterministic tool.

## Core content

### Knowledge boundaries — what it doesn't know

- **Knowledge cutoff.** A model is trained up to a point in time and doesn't know what happened after, including new tool versions and APIs. The effective cutoff even varies by topic, so "it knew about X" is no guarantee it knows about Y.
- **No real-time data.** Prices, today's news, your latest build status — the raw model has none of it unless it can search or call a tool.
- **No access to your private world.** It cannot see your systems, repositories, internal docs, or screen, and it doesn't remember a conversation you had yesterday. If it needs that, you have to supply it (see Context Engineering).

### Accuracy boundaries — what it gets wrong

- **Fabricated specifics.** Facts, citations, numbers, and names can be invented and stated confidently (see Hallucinations).
- **Exact math and counting.** A language model is not a calculator. It predicts plausible text rather than computing, so it grows unreliable as numbers get larger and struggles even to count items or characters precisely. Offload arithmetic and exact logic to code or a real calculator.

### Reasoning boundaries — where thinking breaks down

- **Fragile multi-step reasoning.** Research suggests current models often replicate reasoning patterns seen in training rather than reasoning from first principles; in one well-known study, simply adding an irrelevant-but-plausible sentence to a math problem caused large drops in accuracy. Treat complex logic as something to check, not trust.
- **Novel problems.** It is strongest on things close to its training data and weakest on genuinely new situations.
- **Long or complex interactions degrade.** Quality drops over very long contexts and meandering multi-turn chats (see Context Windows and Prompt vs. Conversation).

### Behavioural boundaries — how it behaves

- **No true understanding.** It is pattern prediction, not comprehension (see What AI Is).
- **Inconsistency.** The same prompt can produce different answers; outputs are not reproducible.
- **Phrasing sensitivity.** Small wording changes can shift the result.
- **Bias.** It can reflect skews in its training data (see Bias in AI).
- **Overconfidence.** It sounds equally sure whether right or wrong, and can't reliably judge its own correctness (see Evaluating AI Outputs).

### What to do about the limits

- Match the task to the right tool. Push exact math and logic into code; use search or connected tools for current information; supply private context yourself.
- Keep a human on genuine judgment and novel reasoning (see AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment).
- Expect failure at the limits and verify there specifically.

A caveat that cuts both ways: when a model is paired with tools — running code for math, searching the web for recent facts — several of these limits shrink. The limitations describe the raw model; knowing them is also how you know which tools to reach for.

## Examples

- **Counting.** "How many test cases are in this file?" — don't trust the number. Ask it to list them and count yourself, or run a script.
- **Recency.** Relying on it for the newest Playwright API can give you a confidently outdated answer; check the current docs.
- **Private behaviour.** "How does our app behave under load?" — it cannot see your system; it will guess.

## Common pitfalls

- Using AI as a calculator or counter. Offload exact computation.
- Trusting it on recent or version-specific details. The cutoff bites here.
- Assuming it knows your private context. It doesn't unless you provide it.
- Expecting reproducible output. It's probabilistic.
- Reading confidence as capability. Sounding sure is not the same as being able.

## Key takeaways

- AI's blind spots are predictable: exact math, current/private info, genuine multi-step reasoning, and consistency.
- Offload what it's bad at to deterministic tools; keep judgment with a human.
- Tools (code execution, search) shrink several limits — knowing the limits tells you when to use them.
- Confidence is not capability; expect and verify failure at the edges.
