# Understanding Tool Limitations and Documentation

## In one line

Beyond the general limits of AI, every specific tool has its own documented limits — usage caps, context size, supported features, data terms, and a model card stating what it's for and what it wasn't tested on — and the official docs, not the chatbot, are where you find them.

## Why this matters for QA

QA lives by specs — reading the documentation before relying on something is a native instinct, and it applies directly to AI tools. Knowing a tool's documented limits prevents the avoidable surprises: hitting a message cap mid-task, a context limit silently truncating your input, or pasting data into a tool whose terms don't actually protect it. Reading the docs is the same discipline as reading requirements before you test — five minutes up front saves an afternoon of confusion.

## Core content

### Tool-specific limits vs. general AI limits

*Practical Limitations of AI* covered what AI can't do well in general — exact math, knowledge cutoff, fragile reasoning. This chapter is about what *this specific tool* will and won't do: its documented constraints. Both matter, but these vary tool by tool, so you check them per tool.

### The kinds of limits a tool documents

- **Usage caps / rate limits** — message limits per time window in the app, or requests/tokens per minute on an API. Hit them mid-task and you're blocked or quietly downgraded to a weaker model.
- **Context limit** — how much input it accepts; go over and your input may be silently truncated (see *Context Windows*).
- **Supported inputs, outputs, and features** — file types, image or audio support, and which features exist on *your* tier.
- **Model availability** — which models your plan can actually use.
- **Data-handling terms** — retention, whether inputs train the model, and enterprise protections. This one is a confidentiality decision (see *Confidentiality and Sensitive Data*).
- **Cost / credits** — premium requests, token costs.

### Model cards and system cards

The makers publish a kind of nutrition label for their models: a **model card** (the trained model) or **system card** (the deployed product) that documents intended use, capabilities, **limitations**, evaluation results, risks, and — importantly — **out-of-scope uses**: "here's what we didn't test it on; if you use it for that, you're on your own." It's worth reading at least the limitations and safety sections. They tell you where a tool is likely to fail before you find out the hard way.

### Read the docs — not the chatbot

Don't ask the AI about its own limits and trust the answer. A model often doesn't accurately know its own current version, features, or limits — a mix of knowledge cutoff and the fact that it can't reliably report on itself (see *Evaluating AI Outputs* and *Model Versions*). Go to the official documentation, the tool's settings, or your admin. Treat the docs as the spec.

### Make it a habit

Before you rely on a tool for real work, skim its docs for the limits that affect your task: context size, usage caps, data terms, supported features. It's the same move as reading requirements before testing — and it pays off the same way.

## Examples

- A long spec gets pasted and the tool silently drops the middle (context limit) → the analysis misses half the document. Knowing the limit, you'd have chunked it.
- You plan a big batch of generations and hit a message cap halfway → check usage limits first.
- You assume image upload works, but it's not on your tier → check supported features.
- Before pasting client data, you read the data-handling terms (or the approved-tool list) instead of assuming it's safe.
- You ask the AI "what's your context window?" and it confidently gives an outdated number → check the docs instead.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Pick the AI tool you use most and find, in its official documentation, three things: its context limit, its usage or message limits, and its data-handling terms. You'll probably learn at least one thing that changes how you use it.

## Common pitfalls

- **Confusing general AI limits with this tool's specific limits.**
- **Asking the AI about its own limits and trusting it.**
- **Assuming a feature or tier capability you don't actually have.**
- **Discovering a usage cap or context limit mid-task.**
- **Skipping the data-handling terms before sharing sensitive content.**

## Key takeaways

- Each tool has documented limits beyond AI's general ones — check them per tool.
- Know the usual suspects: usage caps, context size, features, data terms, cost.
- Read the model/system card's limitations and out-of-scope sections.
- Trust the official docs over the chatbot's account of itself.
