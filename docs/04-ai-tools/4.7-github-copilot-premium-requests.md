# GitHub Copilot Premium Requests

> **⚠ Billing changed — and changes often.** On **June 1, 2026**, GitHub Copilot moved from *premium requests* to **usage-based billing with GitHub AI Credits**. "Premium requests" is now the **legacy** system, kept only for existing annual Pro/Pro+ subscribers until their plan expires. Exact allowances, prices, and multipliers shift frequently — treat every number here as illustrative and **verify on GitHub's official billing pages** before making decisions. *(Consider retitling this chapter "Copilot Usage & Billing" on the portal.)*

## In one line

Copilot meters your advanced AI usage against a monthly allowance — now measured in GitHub AI Credits by tokens consumed (formerly in "premium requests") — so the model you pick and how much work you ask for directly determine how fast you burn through it.

## Why this matters for QA

Copilot isn't a flat "use it all you want" tool for its advanced features, and treating it like one is how a developer burns a month's allowance before lunch. The durable lesson underneath the billing details is simple: **which model you choose is a cost decision**, whether or not you realize it. Defaulting to the most powerful model for every little task is expensive and usually unnecessary. Knowing how the meter works lets you — and the colleagues you support — get the value without the surprise bill.

## Core content

### What's metered, and what's free

The important split, which has stayed constant through the billing change: basic **code completions and next-edit suggestions remain free and unlimited** on paid plans. What consumes your allowance is the *advanced* work — Copilot Chat, agent mode, multi-file edits, code review assistance, and anything using a frontier model. If you mostly use gray-text autocomplete in your editor, your usage barely moves.

### The current model: GitHub AI Credits (from June 1, 2026)

Each plan includes a monthly allowance of **AI Credits**, and usage is metered by **token consumption** (input, output, and cached tokens) at each model's published rate. Because it's token-based, cost is proportional to *work*: a quick question on a lightweight model costs a fraction of a credit; a long agent session on a frontier model across many files costs far more. When your credits (and any budget you've set) run out, usage is blocked or billed as overage — there's no longer a free fallback to a cheaper model.

### The legacy model: premium requests (annual plans only)

Before June 2026, and still for existing **annual** Pro/Pro+ subscribers until their plan ends, billing worked in **premium requests**: each interaction cost one request, multiplied by a **model multiplier** based on the model's power (a stronger model might cost several requests per interaction, a lighter one a fraction). Each plan had a fixed monthly allowance of premium requests, with overage priced per request. Multipliers increased for these annual subscribers on June 1, 2026. If that's you, model choice matters even more directly — a heavier model drains the allowance several times faster.

### The through-line: model choice is a cost decision

Whether you're on credits or legacy requests, the same principle holds: **the model you pick drives the cost.** Frontier models (the most capable) consume dramatically more than lightweight ones for the same task. Most everyday work — routine chat, simple edits — doesn't need the biggest model. Reaching for the most powerful model by default isn't a quality decision, it's an (unintended) cost decision (see *How to Choose the Right AI Tool* and *Model Versions*). Token-efficient prompting helps too, since usage is now literally token-based (see *Token-Efficient Prompting Strategies*).

### Managing usage

- **Pick the right model for the task**, and consider **auto model selection** where offered — it lets the tool choose a sensible (often cheaper) model.
- **Monitor your usage** — the VS Code status bar and GitHub's billing pages show how much of your allowance you've used.
- **Set a budget** to cap overage spend and get alerts before you hit the limit.
- **For teams/admins**, model-access and budget controls prevent a few heavy users (or one expensive model) from draining the pool.

## Examples

- **Defaulting to the most powerful model for every chat** → your allowance drains fast; most of those questions a lighter model handles fine.
- **A long agent session across many files on a frontier model** → a real chunk of credits in one go; worth doing deliberately, not by reflex.
- **Plain autocomplete all day** → essentially free; the meter barely moves.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Open Copilot's usage view (in VS Code's status bar or your GitHub billing page) and note how much of your monthly allowance you've used — and on which features. Then do the same task once on a frontier model and once on a lighter one and compare the draw. The difference is the cost decision you make every time you pick a model.

## Common pitfalls

- **Assuming everything is unlimited.** Advanced features are metered; completions aren't.
- **Defaulting to the most powerful model.** It's the fastest way to burn your allowance.
- **Not knowing which billing model you're on.** Annual plans may still be on legacy premium requests.
- **Ignoring the meter until it runs out.** There's no free fallback now — monitor and budget.
- **Trusting specific numbers from any guide.** Verify current allowances and rates on GitHub.

## Key takeaways

- Copilot meters advanced AI use; basic code completions stay free.
- Since June 2026 it's usage-based (AI Credits by tokens); premium requests are legacy for annual plans.
- Model choice — and prompt/token efficiency — directly drive how fast you consume your allowance.
- Monitor usage, set budgets, and verify current figures on GitHub's official pages.
