# Giving Feedback to Improve Responses Within a Conversation

## In one line

When a response isn't right, steer it with a specific, directional follow-up — say what you want changed and how, not just that it's wrong — because vague dissatisfaction gives the model nothing to act on.

### Why this matters for QA

Most of your AI work happens mid-conversation, and the difference between "that's not what I wanted" and a precise redirect is the difference between three wasted turns and one good one. This is the same skill as writing a useful bug report: "it doesn't work" helps nobody; "expected X, got Y, here's where" gets it fixed. There's a subtlety too — the model tends to *cave* when you push back, so feedback has to steer rather than just express displeasure, or you'll get agreement instead of improvement.

## Core content

### Steer, don't just react

The most effective move on weak output is a specific follow-up direction, not deleting the chat and starting over. Models work well as iterative partners — they use the conversation so far to refine. "Make this shorter," "use a neutral tone," "focus only on the edge cases" are all more useful than "that's not right."

### Say what you want, not just what's wrong

Vague dissatisfaction ("this isn't good," "try again") makes the model guess — and it often changes the wrong thing. Name the gap and the direction:

- **Weak:** "That's not what I meant."
- **Strong:** "You focused on performance testing; I need functional coverage of the checkout flow instead."
- **Weak:** "Too long."
- **Strong:** "Cut it to five bullets, keep the edge cases, drop the introduction."

The pattern is *what to change + how + what to keep*.

### Preserve what worked

If part of the answer is good, say so specifically — otherwise the next version may throw it away. "Keep the table structure and the first three cases, but replace the last two with negative scenarios." This protects progress across turns.

### Add the missing context instead of just correcting

Often a wrong answer means the model lacked information, not that it reasoned badly. Supplying the missing rule or constraint fixes the cause rather than the symptom: "It also enforces a 15-minute lockout after five failures — redo the cases with that rule" (see *Context Engineering*).

### Don't argue it into agreement

Here's the caveat that makes this chapter more than "be specific." Models tend toward agreement, and research shows they often endorse a user's pushback *even when the user is wrong* (see *AI as a Peer Reviewer*). So:

- If you're **correcting a fact**, provide the correct information rather than just asserting it's wrong.
- If you're **testing its reasoning**, ask it to explain the basis for its answer rather than telling it it's wrong and waiting for a fold. A caved answer isn't a corrected one.
- Treat instant capitulation as a signal of nothing, not a confirmation you were right.

### Know when to stop steering and restart

Feedback is for course-correction. When the conversation has stopped converging — you're re-explaining the same thing for the third time — consolidate what you've learned into a clean prompt and start fresh (see *Prompt vs. Conversation* and *Prompt Iteration*).

### Two different things called "feedback"

Don't confuse them:

- **In-conversation feedback** (this chapter) — changes the *next* response, immediately. This is your working lever.
- **Thumbs up/down** in the product — goes to the provider's training and evaluation datasets on their schedule. It does *not* change how the model answers you now, or later in this chat. Useful for the ecosystem, useless for your current task.

And remember it's all temporary: the model doesn't permanently learn from your corrections (see *How AI Models Learn*). Feedback that keeps proving useful belongs in a saved prompt (see *Personal Prompt Library*).

## Examples

**Weak exchange.** "Write test cases for login." → generic output → "No, that's wrong." → the model apologizes and produces something differently generic.

**Strong exchange.** "Write test cases for login." → generic output → "Good structure, keep the table format. Two gaps: you missed lockout after five failed attempts, and every case is happy-path. Regenerate with the lockout rule and add negative cases for invalid credentials and malformed email." → a targeted, better set.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Next time an answer misses, write your follow-up in the *what to change + how + what to keep* shape instead of "that's not right." Compare how many turns it takes to get where you wanted — usually noticeably fewer.

## Common pitfalls

- **Vague dissatisfaction.** "Make it better" gives nothing to act on.
- **Not saying what to keep.** The good parts get discarded.
- **Correcting by assertion.** Supply the right information, or it may just agree.
- **Reading a fast capitulation as confirmation.** That's sycophancy, not verification.
- **Endlessly patching a derailed chat** instead of restarting clean.
- **Expecting thumbs-up/down to change this conversation.** It won't.

## Key takeaways

- Steer with specific, directional follow-ups rather than restarting or complaining.
- Use *what to change + how + what to keep*, and supply missing context rather than just correcting.
- Don't argue the model into agreement — a caved answer proves nothing.
- In-conversation feedback works now; thumbs-up/down only feeds the provider's training.
