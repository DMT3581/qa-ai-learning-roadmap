# Explaining AI-Assisted Work Transparently to Colleagues

## In one line

Be open about how AI contributed to your work — but know that disclosure carries a real, measured social cost, that being *caught* concealing it costs more, and that the only thing which reliably removes the penalty is a team culture where AI use is normal.

### Why this matters for QA

Colleagues review your work, and how it was produced changes how they should review it — a test plan you wrote from scratch and one AI drafted from a spec warrant different scrutiny. Transparency isn't a virtue badge; it's functional information for the people downstream. But this chapter would be dishonest if it stopped there, because research shows openness about AI use often *lowers* how competent and trustworthy colleagues judge you. Knowing that tension — and how it actually resolves — is more useful to you, and to the colleagues you support, than a cheerful instruction to always disclose.

## Core content

### Why it matters functionally

- **It calibrates review.** A reviewer who knows AI drafted something checks the things AI gets wrong — invented specifics, missing domain rules.
- **It protects trust.** Work quietly passed off as fully hand-made, then discovered, damages credibility more than the AI use ever would have.
- **It spreads knowledge.** "Here's the prompt I used" teaches the team something; silence teaches nothing (see *Documenting and Sharing Effective Prompts*).
- **It keeps accountability visible.** Disclosure says how the work was made — not that someone else is responsible for it.

### The uncomfortable finding: the transparency dilemma

Here's what the evidence actually says. Across 13 experiments with over 5,000 participants, people who disclosed using generative AI were trusted **less** than those who didn't — traced not to the work's quality but to a perceived loss of *legitimacy*, a lingering expectation that real work reflects human effort. A 2026 controlled experiment with ~960 knowledge workers found the same: evaluating *identical* work, colleagues rated the AI-disclosing author far lazier and were substantially less likely to recommend them for a high-visibility project. Tweaking how you word the disclosure barely helps, and being AI-savvy yourself doesn't make you judge others more kindly.

So the naive advice — "just be transparent, people will respect it" — is not what the data shows.

### Why you should still be open

Two things resolve the dilemma:

- **Being exposed is worse than disclosing.** The same research found that undisclosed AI use discovered by someone else damages trust *more* than voluntary disclosure. Transparency is the lesser cost — and the safer bet as AI use becomes easier to spot.
- **Culture removes most of the penalty.** In workplaces where AI use is treated as normal and legitimate, the penalty largely disappears — in the 2026 study, in teams that actively embraced AI, the "laziness" penalty nearly vanished and disclosers were seen as *more efficient*.

That second point is the actionable one, and it's your job: **the penalty is a norms problem, and ambassadors change norms.** Every time AI use is discussed openly and matter-of-factly in your team, the cost of being open goes down for everyone.

### Disclose proportionally

Not everything needs a note. Match the disclosure to how much AI shaped the outcome:

- **Usually no need:** autocomplete, spelling and grammar help, using AI to understand something you then wrote yourself.
- **Worth mentioning:** AI drafted a deliverable, generated the test cases, or did analysis that others will rely on.
- **Definitely disclose:** it materially shaped a decision or a client-facing artifact — and where a policy or client contract requires it (see *IP and Copyright* and *AI Ethics in Everyday Work*).

### How to phrase it

Matter-of-fact, and focused on **what you did with the output** — this signals the human judgment that's actually in question:

- ✅ "I generated a first pass with AI, then reviewed each case against the spec and added the lockout scenarios it missed."
- ❌ "Sorry, I just used AI for this." (Apologetic — invites the penalty.)
- ❌ "AI wrote it." (Vague, and sounds like no one checked.)
- ❌ Over-narrating every trivial use, which turns into noise.

Naming your verification is the honest part *and* the part that answers the legitimacy concern.

### Disclosure isn't a transfer of responsibility

Saying "AI drafted this" does not make AI accountable for it. You still own what you ship (see *AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment*). Transparency describes the process; it doesn't move the responsibility.

## Examples

- **Sharing a test plan:** "AI drafted the structure from the spec; I rewrote the risk section and verified the coverage." → the reviewer knows where to look hardest.
- **In a stand-up:** "I used AI to triage the failure logs — it grouped them, I confirmed the root cause." → normal, unremarkable, and it normalizes the practice.
- **Client deliverable:** check the contract and policy before deciding; disclosure may be required (see *IP and Copyright*).

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Next time AI helped meaningfully, mention it in one plain sentence that includes what you verified. Notice how the conversation goes — and notice that the more often it happens in your team, the less remarkable it becomes. That's the culture shift, happening in the small moments.

## Common pitfalls

- **Concealing meaningful AI involvement.** Discovery costs more than disclosure.
- **Apologizing for it.** Framing it as a confession invites the penalty.
- **Over-disclosing trivial use.** Every autocomplete doesn't need a footnote.
- **Disclosing without saying what you verified.** That's the part colleagues care about.
- **Treating disclosure as a transfer of accountability.** It isn't.
- **Ignoring client-contract or policy requirements.**

## Key takeaways

- Transparency is functional: it tells colleagues how to review, and protects trust.
- Research shows disclosure carries a real trust and competence penalty — being open is genuinely costly.
- But concealment discovered is worse, and a normalizing culture largely removes the penalty.
- Disclose proportionally, state what you verified, and remember you still own the result.
