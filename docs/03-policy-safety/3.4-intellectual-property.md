## In one line

AI raises unsettled IP questions on both sides — what you feed in and what comes out — and the two facts to carry are that you may not *own* purely AI-generated output and that it can sometimes *reproduce* protected material, so route the specifics to legal and the client contract.

## Why this matters for QA

You work on deliverables for external clients, where IP is contractual and taken seriously. Two things can go wrong: feeding in material you don't have the rights to share, and shipping AI output whose IP status is murky — output you may not own, or that may reproduce someone else's protected code or text. In a consulting relationship, getting this wrong can breach a contract, not just cause embarrassment. Because this is an evolving, country-dependent legal area, the goal here is to recognize the risks and route them to the right people — not to become a lawyer.

## Core content

### Two sides: what goes in, what comes out

- **Input:** don't feed AI material you don't have the rights to — client code under IP terms, licensed content, copyrighted text. That's an IP issue as well as a confidentiality one (see *Confidentiality and Sensitive Data*).
- **Output:** the less-understood, murkier side — who owns, and who might be infringed by, what the AI produces.

### You may not own purely AI-generated output

Copyright generally requires **human authorship**. In the US, the Copyright Office and the courts have held that purely AI-generated output — even from a long, detailed prompt — is not copyrightable, and that entering a prompt does not by itself make you the author. What *is* protectable is the meaningful human contribution: your creative editing, selection, arrangement, and modification of the output. The more you genuinely shape the result, the stronger the claim — and it's assessed case-by-case and still being litigated.

Two practical consequences: other countries take somewhat different positions (some have recognized copyright in AI-assisted works with sufficient human creativity), so **jurisdiction matters** with international clients; and a tool's own terms may or may not assign output rights to you, so check them. The upshot for deliverables: AI-generated content may not be protectable the way hand-authored work is, which matters when a client expects to own exclusive rights.

### AI output can sometimes infringe

Because models are trained on large bodies of existing work, output can occasionally reproduce or closely resemble protected material. Infringement risk arises where the model had access to a work and produced something substantially similar to it. For **code**, this shows up as output that mirrors training data or carries a restrictive/copyleft license, which can "contaminate" a client codebase's licensing if merged in. Some coding tools offer duplicate-detection filters and IP indemnity — know what yours offers and how it's set. Either way, you and the firm are responsible for what you ship; fair use is narrow and unlikely to rescue commercial output that infringes.

### Client contracts and disclosure

In consulting, IP lives in the contract. Clients often require deliverables to be original, owned, and free of restrictive-license encumbrance, and may require that AI use be disclosed — or restrict it outright. AI use can quietly conflict with those terms, and failing to disclose where disclosure is expected is itself a problem. Check the contract and firm policy *before* using AI on a client deliverable, and disclose when required.

### It's unsettled — defer appropriately

This area is evolving quickly, differs by country, and turns on case-by-case facts, so treat none of it as settled. When it matters, route the question to legal, your IP policy, and the client contract. Don't rely on the model's opinion about its own output's copyright, and don't use AI as your legal advisor.

### Practical guidance

- Don't input material you lack the rights to.
- Add meaningful human authorship to anything you need to own.
- For code: know your tool's filter and indemnity; review AI code for license and provenance; don't merge unknown-provenance code into client repos.
- Check the client contract and firm policy; disclose AI use when required.
- When unsure, ask legal — this is a route-it, not guess-it, question.

## Examples

- **Automation code** from AI that closely matches a licensed snippet → possible license contamination of the client repo; review provenance before merging.
- **Client-facing docs** written with AI → check whether the contract requires original/owned work and whether AI use must be disclosed.
- **Treating AI output as "yours"** on a deliverable → it may not be copyrightable without your creative input; add real human authorship.

## Common pitfalls

- **Assuming you own whatever AI generates.** Purely AI-generated output often isn't copyrightable.
- **Assuming AI output can't infringe.** It sometimes can, and you're responsible.
- **Inputting copyrighted or licensed material** you don't have rights to.
- **Merging AI code without checking license and provenance.**
- **Ignoring client-contract IP and disclosure terms.**
- **Treating the law as settled — or asking the AI for the legal answer.**

## Key takeaways

- Purely AI-generated output generally isn't yours to own without meaningful human authorship.
- AI output can occasionally infringe; code carries license-contamination risk.
- IP is contractual in consulting — check the contract and disclose AI use when required.
- The law is unsettled and country-dependent; route specifics to legal, not the model.
