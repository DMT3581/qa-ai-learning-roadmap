## In one line

Everyday AI ethics isn't abstract philosophy — it's a handful of practical habits (be transparent about AI use, own what you produce, protect people's data, watch for bias, don't deceive) that keep your work honest and trustworthy.

## Why this matters for QA

You make small ethical choices with AI more often than you'd think — whether to tell a colleague AI drafted something, whether to run client data through a tool, whether to lean on an output that affects a release. None of these need a philosophy degree; they need a few clear principles and the habit of applying them. For a consulting firm, your credibility with clients rests on doing this well — cutting an ethical corner with AI is a fast way to lose trust.

## Core content

### The principles, in plain terms

The major frameworks — the OECD AI Principles, the UNESCO Recommendation on the Ethics of AI, and the NIST AI Risk Management Framework — converge on the same short list: transparency, accountability, fairness, privacy, human oversight, and doing no harm. This chapter is about applying them in the small moments, not debating them in the abstract.

### Be transparent about AI use

Don't pass off AI-generated work as your own where it matters, and disclose AI use when it's relevant — a client deliverable, a review, a shared artifact. The frameworks explicitly call for making people aware when they're relying on or interacting with AI, including in the workplace. Where disclosure is expected, staying silent is itself a form of deception. (See *Intellectual Property* on contract terms and *Explaining AI-Assisted Work Transparently* on team transparency.)

### Own what you produce

AI doesn't displace your responsibility — every framework stresses that ultimate accountability stays human. You're answerable for AI-assisted output as if you'd made it yourself; "the AI did it" is not a defense (see *AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment*). That means verifying before you ship (see *Evaluating AI Outputs*) and not using AI to manufacture confidence or expertise you don't actually have.

### Protect people's data and privacy

Whose data are you putting in? Personal data, client data, a colleague's information — consent and confidentiality still apply, and using AI usually *heightens* the obligation rather than suspending it (see *Confidentiality and Sensitive Data*).

### Be fair — watch for bias

AI can reflect and amplify bias from its training data (see *Bias in AI*). Where an output affects people or priorities, be alert to unfair skew — and don't launder a questionable call through "the AI said so."

### Don't use AI to deceive or fake the work

Fabricating results or evidence, faking competence, generating content meant to mislead, or skipping checks you're responsible for — AI makes all of these easier, and they're all still wrong. The line is the integrity of the work: use AI to do the work *better*, not to fake having done it.

### Keep a human on decisions that affect people

Meaningful human oversight on anything with real stakes or human impact runs through every framework. Automate the mechanics; don't abdicate the judgment.

### Wider costs, briefly

People increasingly weigh broader dimensions too — the environmental footprint of heavy AI use, the data and labour behind the models, and over-reliance. You don't have to resolve these debates, but using AI deliberately rather than wastefully is part of responsible use.

### Gray areas, and where the floor is

Ethics isn't always clear-cut, and reasonable people differ. A simple gut-check covers most cases: *would I be comfortable if the client, my manager, and the person affected knew exactly how I used AI here?* When it's genuinely unclear or high-stakes, raise it. Company policy, professional norms, and law set the floor; your judgment fills in the rest.

## Examples

- **AI drafts a test report going to the client** → disclose per the contract/policy, and own its accuracy.
- **A colleague asks if you wrote a doc yourself** → be honest that AI helped.
- **Tempted to have AI "generate" test evidence you didn't actually run** → that's fabrication; don't.
- **AI-assisted defect prioritization that consistently buries accessibility bugs** → check for bias before acting on it.

## Common pitfalls

- **Passing off AI work as your own** where disclosure matters.
- **"The AI did it"** used as an accountability dodge.
- **Running personal or client data through AI** without regard to privacy.
- **Using AI to fake results, evidence, or competence.**
- **Laundering an unfair call through "the AI said so."**
- **Treating ethics as someone else's job.** Policy sets the floor; you apply it.

## Key takeaways

- The frameworks agree on a short list: transparency, accountability, fairness, privacy, oversight, no harm.
- Be honest about AI use, and own the output as if you'd made it.
- Protect people's data and watch for bias in anything that affects people.
- Use AI to do the work better, never to fake it — and raise the gray areas.
