## In one line

Treat every AI tool as an external party: never put anything into it that you would not be allowed to email to someone outside the company — unless it is an approved, contractually protected enterprise instance.

## Why this matters for QA

QA work is full of exactly the data that must not leak: client source code under NDA, test data containing real personal information, bug reports that describe exploitable weaknesses, logs and stack traces with credentials, and requirement documents covered by client agreements. Pasting any of this into the wrong AI tool can breach a client NDA, violate data-protection law (e.g., GDPR), and expose the company to real legal and reputational damage. As a consulting firm working on external clients' systems, our obligation is not only to our own confidentiality but to theirs — and that obligation usually survives in writing long after a project ends.

## Core content

### The mental model: an AI tool is an external service

When you type into an AI tool, your text leaves your machine and is processed on someone else's servers. Before sharing anything, ask two questions:

1. **Where does this data go, and who could see it?** (The provider's staff, sub-processors, other users in shared workspaces, logs.)
2. **Could it be stored or used to train a model?** If so, fragments of it could resurface later, outside your control.

If you cannot answer both questions confidently for the tool in front of you, treat it as unsafe for anything non-public.

### What you must NOT share (default-deny)

Unless you are using an approved enterprise tool **and** the data is permitted by client/company rules, do not put the following into any AI tool:

- **Client source code and proprietary code** covered by an NDA or IP terms.
- **Personal data / PII** — names, emails, phone numbers, addresses, national IDs, payment data, health data, anything that identifies a real person. (This is the data most regulated by law.)
- **Credentials and secrets** — passwords, API keys, tokens, certificates, connection strings, `.env` contents. These should never be in a prompt, period.
- **Internal infrastructure details** — non-public URLs, IP addresses, server names, architecture diagrams, security configurations.
- **Confidential business information** — unreleased plans, contracts, pricing, financials, anything marked confidential.
- **Security findings that could be weaponized** — exploit details, vulnerability write-ups tied to a live client system.

> A model does not understand what is sensitive — it only processes patterns. It will not protect a secret for you; that is entirely your responsibility before you hit send.

### What is generally safe

- Genuinely public information (published docs, open-source code, public standards).
- Fully **anonymized or synthetic** data with no link back to a real person or client.
- **Generic, decoupled** technical questions ("how do I structure a Playwright test for a login flow?") that carry no client context.
- Your own non-sensitive drafts, notes, and learning questions.

### The tier-and-settings trap

The single biggest source of confusion is assuming "Tool X is safe" or "Tool X is unsafe." The truth depends on **which version** you use and **how it is configured**:

- **Consumer tiers** (free/personal plans) frequently allow providers to retain conversations and use them to improve models — sometimes by default, sometimes after an opt-out you have to find and set.
- **Business / enterprise / API tiers** are typically governed by commercial terms that contractually exclude your inputs from model training and offer stronger retention and access controls.

These defaults also **change over time**, so a "fact" you learned last year may no longer hold. The practical rule: use only the company-approved enterprise instances for any work-related content, and re-verify the policy periodically rather than trusting memory.

### How to use AI on sensitive work — safely

You rarely need the real sensitive data to get value from AI. Options, from simplest to strongest:

1. **Abstract the problem.** Ask the general question without the real data ("how do I assert on a paginated API response?") instead of pasting the live response with a customer's record in it.
2. **Sanitize.** Replace real identifiers with placeholders (`user@example.com`, `CUSTOMER_ID`, `<API_KEY>`), strip secrets, and remove client names before sharing.
3. **Use synthetic data.** Generate representative fake data that has the same shape as the real thing but identifies no one.
4. **Use only approved tools** for anything beyond fully public content.
5. **When in doubt, don't.** A blocked task is recoverable; a leaked client secret often is not.

### QA-specific traps to watch

- **Real production data in test environments** — test fixtures often contain real customer records. Sanitize before sharing.
- **Logs, stack traces, and screenshots** — frequently contain tokens, internal URLs, or PII. Scrub them first.
- **Bug reports** — a clear bug report can be a step-by-step exploit guide; keep client-specific security detail out of public tools.
- **Whole-repository pastes** — pulling an entire client repo into a tool to "ask about it" can expose far more than you intend.

## Examples

**Weak (do not do this):**
> "This API call fails: `POST /api/users` with body `{"email":"maria.popescu@acme-client.com","ssn":"...","token":"sk-live-9f2a..."}` — why do I get a 500?"

This leaks a real customer's PII, a client identity, and a live secret in a single message.

**Strong (sanitized and abstracted):**
> "A `POST /api/users` endpoint returns 500 when the request body includes an unexpected field. Here is a sanitized example with placeholder values: `{"email":"user@example.com","id":"<USER_ID>"}`. What server-side causes should I check, and how would I write a Playwright test to reproduce it?"

Same help, no exposure.

**Prompt example**
- **Goal:** Get AI help debugging a failing test without exposing client data.
- **Prompt:** "Here is a failing assertion with all identifiers replaced by placeholders: [sanitized snippet]. Suggest likely causes and a fix."
- **Why it works:** The model sees the structure of the problem, which is all it needs, while the sensitive content never leaves your control.

## Common pitfalls

- **"It's just a quick question."** → Speed is the most common reason secrets leak. The checklist takes ten seconds.
- **"I'm logged into the company account, so it's safe."** → Verify the account is actually an approved enterprise tier with training disabled; consumer plans under a work email are still consumer plans.
- **"I'll remove the sensitive bits after."** → Once sent, it has already left. Sanitize before, not after.
- **"The AI promised it won't remember."** → In-chat reassurances from a model are not a data-protection guarantee. Trust the contract and settings, not the chatbot.

## Key takeaways

- Default-deny: if it's not public, synthetic, or approved, don't share it.
- Never put credentials, secrets, or real PII into any AI tool.
- Tier and settings decide the risk, and they change — use approved enterprise instances and re-verify.
- You can almost always get the same help by abstracting or sanitizing the problem first.
