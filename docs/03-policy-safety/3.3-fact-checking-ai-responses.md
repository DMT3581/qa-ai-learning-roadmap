## In one line

AI states facts — and fake citations — with the same confidence as real ones, so check each claim against an independent source, and never trust the AI's own references without confirming they exist and actually say what it claims.

## Why this matters for QA

You'll use AI to answer factual questions you then act on: "does Playwright support this?", "what does this standard require?", "what's the correct config for that?". AI delivers the answer fluently whether it's correct or invented — sometimes with citations that look authoritative but don't exist. In a consulting firm that delivers to external clients, acting on a fabricated fact or a made-up "best practice" is a direct credibility risk. *Evaluating AI Outputs* told you *when* to verify; this chapter is the *how* — the verification half of using AI for anything you'll rely on or hand to a client.

## Core content

### Why facts need an external check

A model predicts plausible text; it has no mechanism to confirm that what it says is true. So treat a factual claim from AI as a **lead to verify**, not a settled answer — especially the specifics you'd act on: numbers, dates, names, versions, quotes, and exact tool behaviour. These are precisely where it is least reliable (see *Practical Limitations of AI*).

### The citation trap

The most dangerous version of this is fabricated sources. AI routinely produces references that look completely real — a plausible title, real-sounding authors, even a DOI or URL that resolves, just to a *different* work than the one named. Studies of AI-generated citations have found large fractions either entirely fabricated or carrying errors such as wrong years or page numbers, with the rate climbing for niche topics, recent publications, and open-ended "find me sources" requests.

The trap that catches people: **you cannot verify a citation by asking the AI.** Ask whether a source exists and it will often confirm its own fake and repeat the invented details back to you (the same reason you don't ask a model to grade itself, from *Evaluating AI Outputs*). Verification has to come from *outside* the model. And note a real source cited for a claim it doesn't actually support is just as wrong as a fake one — so check both that the source exists *and* that it says what was claimed.

### How to fact-check — the method

1. **Isolate the checkable claims.** Pull out the specific facts you'll act on, not the prose around them.
2. **Go to authoritative, independent sources.** Official docs, standards, the actual spec or code — not another AI, not a forum.
3. **Confirm the source exists and supports the claim.** Open it and find the actual statement; don't trust the AI's summary of it.
4. **Cross-check for anything important.** Agreement across genuinely independent sources raises confidence; a lone source — or several that all echo one origin — is weak.
5. **Check recency.** For fast-moving things (tools, APIs, prices, laws), confirm the source is current and remember the model's knowledge cutoff.
6. **Prefer primary over secondary.** The official Playwright docs beat a blog summarizing them.

### Source quality, briefly

Primary and official (vendor docs, standards bodies, the spec or code itself) outrank reputable secondary sources (established publications), which outrank random blogs, forums, and AI summaries. Match the rigor of the source to the stakes of the claim.

### Prompt to reduce fabrication

- **Ground it.** Give the model the source and ask it to answer only from that (see *Context Engineering*), then confirm it actually did.
- **Ask for verifiable specifics** you can look up yourself, and tell it to flag uncertainty or say "I don't know" rather than invent.
- **Reframe source requests.** Instead of "give me sources that prove X," ask "where is X documented?" or "what should I check to confirm X?" — then go look.
- **Use web-search/retrieval mode for recency**, but still open and read the cited pages. Retrieval reduces fabrication; it doesn't remove it, because the model can still misread or misattribute a real source.

## Examples

**Weak.** AI says, with a confident documentation-style reference, that a Playwright assertion waits a certain number of seconds by default. You quote it to the team. It's outdated, and the "reference" points to the wrong page.

**Strong.** You treat that as a lead, open the current Playwright docs, confirm the actual behaviour, and cite the docs — not the AI.

**Citation check.** AI gives a "source" with a real-looking URL or DOI for a testing standard. You open it: it 404s, or it resolves to a different document. The believable formatting was not evidence.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Ask AI a factual question in your domain and request two supporting sources. Then verify each one: does it exist, and does it actually say what the AI claimed? Note how many hold up. Doing this once on your own topic is the fastest way to internalize why citations need checking.

## Common pitfalls

- **Trusting AI citations without opening them.** Believable formatting isn't proof.
- **Asking the AI to confirm its own facts or sources.** It will validate its own fabrication.
- **Treating a confident specific as verified.** Numbers, dates, and versions are where it's weakest.
- **Open-ended "find me sources" prompts.** They invite fabrication.
- **Cross-checking with sources that share one origin.** Echoes aren't independent — and AI-written content citing fakes "launders" them.
- **Verifying with another AI.** Check against the real world, not a second model.

## Key takeaways

- Treat AI facts as leads to verify, not settled answers — especially specifics.
- AI citations are frequently fabricated and look real; confirm existence *and* support, from outside the model.
- Cross-check important claims against independent, primary sources, and mind recency.
- Ground prompts in real sources and ask for verifiable specifics to reduce fabrication.
