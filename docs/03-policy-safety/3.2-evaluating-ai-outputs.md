## In one line

AI sounds exactly as confident when it's wrong as when it's right, so trust has to come from checking the work — and you check harder the more it would cost to be wrong.

## Why this matters for QA

Evaluating whether something meets expectations is the core of QA — you already do this for software all day. The trap with AI is that its output is fluent, well-structured, and confident, and all of that reads as "correct" even when it isn't. Under a deadline, that polish is exactly what tempts you to accept it without checking. The skill here is not "verify everything" (you'd never ship) or "trust everything" (you'd ship broken work) — it's calibrating: trusting cheap, reversible output and rigorously verifying anything that matters.

## Core content

### Fluency is not accuracy

A model produces the most statistically plausible text; it has no built-in way to check what it says against reality. It is not "knowing" an answer — it is predicting one. As a result it can be **confidently wrong**: a fluent, authoritative-sounding answer that is simply false. The confidence in the wording tells you nothing about whether the content is correct. Treat tone as style, not evidence.

### Don't ask the model to grade itself

A tempting shortcut is to ask "are you sure?" or "how confident are you?" This does not work as verification. Models are not calibrated to judge the correctness of their own output, and the way you phrase the question can change the confidence they report. Worse, prompting a model to reconsider can sometimes make a correct answer *worse*. Self-assessment is not a substitute for an external check. If you need to know whether an answer is right, verify it against something outside the model.

### The core rule: verify in proportion to the stakes

Match how hard you verify to **how much it would cost to be wrong**, combined with how likely an error is and how hard it would be to catch later.

- **Trust and move on** when the task is low-stakes, easily reversible, and easy to eyeball: brainstorming, first drafts, reformatting, explaining a well-known concept.
- **Verify thoroughly** when being wrong is expensive or hard to undo: anything going to a client, a release or severity decision, a factual claim you'll act on, code that touches production, numbers in a report.

This is the same instinct as risk-based testing — you spend your scrutiny where the risk is.

### Where AI is more vs. less reliable

A rough calibration heuristic:

- **More reliable** — transforming text you gave it (summarizing, reformatting), generating boilerplate, drafting and brainstorming, explaining established concepts.
- **Less reliable** — specific facts, figures, dates, names, and citations; niche or very recent information; anything needing real-time data; precise arithmetic or strict logic; claims about *your* specific systems it cannot see. (See *Practical Limitations of AI*.)

The pattern: AI is strongest when working *on information you provided* and weakest when *recalling specifics from memory*.

### How to verify

- **Check against ground truth.** For factual claims, confirm against a trusted source (see *Fact-Checking AI Responses*).
- **Ground it, then check it used the source.** Give the model the document and ask it to answer from that — then confirm it actually did, rather than drifting into memory.
- **Run it, don't eyeball it.** For code, a regex, a query — execute and test it against valid and invalid inputs. This is the most QA-native check there is: don't trust the snippet, test it.
- **Cross-check for consistency.** Ask again in a fresh chat or with different phrasing; if answers contradict each other, that's a red flag (and a sign not to rely on it).
- **Read for the typical failure modes.** Fabricated specifics, plausible-but-wrong claims, missing edge cases, subtle logic slips (see *Common AI Failure Modes*).
- **Sanity-check against your own expertise.** If it contradicts what you know, your knowledge wins until proven otherwise.

### Mind the bias that makes you skip all this

Over-reliance has a name — automation bias — and it bites hardest when output is fluent and you're short on time. Naming it is the first defence; the verification habits above are the second. (See *AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment*.)

## Examples

**Weak (trusting tone).** The AI states, "Playwright auto-waits for elements, so explicit waits are never needed." It sounds authoritative, you take it as fact, and you ship tests that turn out flaky in edge cases. The confident phrasing was not evidence.

**Strong (verify what matters).** Same claim, but because it affects your test reliability (higher stakes), you check it against the Playwright documentation and confirm the nuance before relying on it.

**Strong (run it).** The AI generates a validation regex. Instead of reading it and assuming it's right, you run it against a list of valid and invalid inputs — turning an unverifiable snippet into a tested one in thirty seconds.

## Common pitfalls

- **Trusting the confident tone.** Polish is not proof.
- **Asking the model if it's sure — and believing it.** Self-assessment isn't verification.
- **One-size verification.** Either over-checking trivia or, worse, under-checking high-stakes output.
- **Verifying AI with the same AI.** That's circular; check against something independent.
- **Skipping the check under deadline.** That's exactly when errors slip through.

## Key takeaways

- Confidence and fluency are not evidence — judge the content, not the tone.
- The model can't reliably grade itself; verify against something external.
- Calibrate verification effort to the cost of being wrong.
- For code and rules, run and test rather than eyeball.
