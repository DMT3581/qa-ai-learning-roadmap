# Setting Realistic Expectations for AI

## In one line

Expect modest, task-specific gains that compound over weeks — not transformation in a day — and expect to get *slower* before you get faster, because that dip is the normal shape of learning, not evidence that AI doesn't work.

### Why this matters for QA

Expectations decide whether people stick with AI or quietly abandon it. Set them too high and the first disappointment reads as proof it's all hype; set them too low and nobody bothers trying. There's a specific trap here: a demo where AI writes something impressive in ten seconds invites you to extrapolate wildly — and your real work is messier, more constrained, and full of context the demo didn't have. As an ambassador, over-promising is the most damaging thing you can do, because the correction is public and it costs your credibility on everything that follows.

## Core content

### The shape of the curve: it dips before it rises

Adopting AI follows a **J-curve**: an initial dip, then gains. You're learning a tool, changing habits, and verifying output you don't yet trust — all of which cost time up front. The single most useful thing to know is that this is normal. Wide evidence points the same way at every scale: a controlled study found experienced developers were about **19% slower** with AI while believing they were faster (see *Measuring Personal Productivity Gains*), and organizationally, most AI initiatives fail to deliver expected benefits, with generative AI now sitting in Gartner's "trough of disillusionment" — the recalibration phase after inflated expectations meet implementation reality.

None of that means AI doesn't work. It means the payoff arrives *after* the learning, and only if you don't quit during the dip.

### It's an old pattern

Economist Robert Solow's line about the computer age — that you could see computers everywhere except in the productivity statistics — describes exactly this. The payoff came later, once organizations learned to work differently. AI is following the same script: **the gain comes from changing how you work, not from having the tool.**

### What realistic actually looks like

- **Task-specific, not global.** Big help on drafting, breadth, boilerplate, and transformation; little or negative on things you're already expert and fast at (see *Cost vs Value*).
- **Modest and compounding.** Twenty minutes saved on a recurring task, several times a week, is a real and typical win. That's what "AI helps" looks like day to day.
- **Weeks, not days.** A habit takes weeks to pay off (see *Integrating AI into Daily Work Habits*), and the compounding comes from your prompt library and workflows maturing, not from the model.
- **Quality gains count too.** Catching a missed edge case or surfacing a gap in a spec is value even when the clock doesn't move.
- **Still requires your work.** The output needs verification; that time is part of the deal, not a sign something's wrong.

### Where inflated expectations come from

- **Demos.** A polished demo shows the best case on a clean problem; your work has messy context and real constraints.
- **Vendor and media framing.** Marketing sells transformation; day-to-day reality is incremental.
- **Feeling faster.** Ease is not speed — the sensation of effortlessness is not a measurement (see *Measuring Personal Productivity Gains*).
- **Extrapolating from one win.** One task going brilliantly doesn't generalize to all tasks.

### Don't overcorrect into cynicism

The opposite failure is just as costly. "AI is useless" after one bad experience is the same error as "AI does everything" — a conclusion drawn from a sample of one. The mature position is calibrated: it's genuinely good at some things, bad at others, and worth measuring rather than believing (see *Evaluating AI Outputs* and *Practical Limitations*).

### What to tell colleagues

Promise less than you expect. Something like: *"It'll probably slow you down the first week. After that, expect it to save you real time on a couple of specific tasks — not on everything."* That prediction survives contact with reality, which means the person still trusts you in month two. Sharing failures alongside wins does the same work (see *Building Team Knowledge*), and honest expectations are also the best defence against the disappointment that hardens into resistance (see *Overcoming Resistance*).

## Examples

- **"AI will halve our test-writing time."** → Overpromise. When it doesn't, the tool gets blamed and abandoned.
- **"AI gives you a solid first draft of test cases; you still review and prune, and that's usually faster overall."** → Realistic, and it holds up.
- **A colleague tries it for a week, finds it slower, and quits** → that's the dip; without knowing it exists, they'll read a normal phase as a verdict.

## Try it yourself *(optional)*

Write down what you expect AI to do for one specific task, and how much time you think it'll save. Then measure it over two weeks (see *Measuring Personal Productivity Gains*). Comparing your prediction to the number is the fastest possible calibration — and it makes you far more credible when colleagues ask what to expect.

## Common pitfalls

- **Extrapolating from demos** to your real, messy work.
- **Expecting immediate gains.** The dip comes first.
- **Quitting during the dip.** That's when most people abandon it.
- **Expecting uniform benefits** across all tasks.
- **Confusing "easier" with "faster."**
- **Over-promising to colleagues.** It costs your credibility, not just theirs.
- **Overcorrecting into "AI is useless"** after one bad experience.

## Key takeaways

- Expect a dip before the gain; the payoff comes after the learning, not instead of it.
- Realistic gains are task-specific, modest, and compounding over weeks.
- The benefit comes from changing how you work, not from having the tool.
- Avoid both hype and cynicism — measure, and promise colleagues less than you expect.
