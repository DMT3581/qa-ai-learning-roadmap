# AI Does Not Replace Human Judgment

## In one line

AI is an advisor, not an authority: it can inform a decision, but the judgment, the final call, and the accountability stay with you.

### Why this matters for QA

QA is fundamentally a judgment role. The mechanical parts — generating cases, drafting reports, summarizing logs — are where AI helps most, but the parts that carry the value are decisions: what is risky enough to test, whether a requirement makes sense, whether a bug is shippable, how to weigh coverage against a deadline. If you hand those calls to an AI, you have given away the part of the job that matters — and you are still accountable for the outcome. "The AI said it was fine" has never been, and will never be, a valid sign-off.

## Core content

### Delegate the work, not the responsibility

You can ask AI to do a task; you cannot ask it to be accountable for the result. Accountability does not transfer to a tool. When you act on an AI's output, you are vouching for it as if you had produced it yourself. That single idea reframes how you should treat every answer: not as a verdict to accept, but as a proposal to evaluate.

### Automation bias: the trap to know about

**Automation bias** is the well-documented human tendency to over-rely on automated systems — to favour their output and discount our own knowledge or contradictory evidence, even when the system is wrong. It has been studied for decades in aviation and medicine, where over-trusting an instrument has contributed to real disasters, and it applies directly to AI tools today.

Two things make it especially easy with modern AI: the output is **fluent and confident**, which reads as competence, and it usually arrives **under time pressure**, when we are most tempted to skip the check. The result shows up as two kinds of error — *commission* (following a wrong recommendation even though it looked questionable) and *omission* (failing to notice when the system got it wrong because you stopped watching). Naming the bias is the first defence against it.

### Where human judgment must stay in the loop

Keep the decision firmly yours whenever any of these are true:

- **High stakes** — being wrong is costly (a release decision, a client-facing deliverable, a security or compliance call).
- **Ambiguity** — the situation needs interpretation, context, or domain knowledge the model does not have.
- **Ethics and people** — anything affecting fairness, privacy, or how people are treated.
- **Irreversibility** — the action is hard to undo.
- **Sign-off** — the moment of approval is, by definition, a human responsibility.

### Where you can lean on AI more

The flip side: when work is **low-stakes, reversible, and easy to verify** — brainstorming, first drafts, reformatting, generating options to choose from — you can rely on AI freely, because a mistake is cheap and you will catch it. Calibrated use means matching how much you trust the output to how much it would cost to be wrong.

### Staying in control (and not deskilling)

Practical habits: treat each output as a draft to be checked against ground truth; before acting, ask yourself *"would I make this call on my own, and can I defend it?"*; and keep exercising your own expertise. There is a long-term risk of **deskilling** — if you stop making judgments yourself, you gradually lose the ability to tell when the AI is wrong, which is exactly the skill that lets you use it safely. Use AI to augment your thinking, not to outsource it.

## Examples

**A QA framing — the severity call.** An AI triages a defect as "low severity, safe to defer." It sounds reasonable, you are behind on the release, so you accept it. The defect was in the payment flow and breaks checkout in production. The model had no real visibility into business impact — but the decision, and the consequences, were yours. The lesson is not "don't use AI to triage"; it is "the AI informs the call, you make it."

**Test cases.** Asking AI to generate 50 test cases is excellent leverage. Accepting all 50 without reading them is not — half may be trivial duplicates and the risky edge case may be missing entirely. The judgment about *what coverage actually matters* is the part only you can supply.

## Common pitfalls

- **"The AI said so."** This is not a defence and not a sign-off. You own what you ship.
- **Rubber-stamping under deadline.** Time pressure is exactly when automation bias bites hardest; that is when to slow down, not speed up.
- **Judgment smuggled in as "just a suggestion."** If you always follow the suggestion, the AI is making the decision — be honest about who is actually deciding.
- **Quiet deskilling.** Outsourcing every judgment erodes the expertise you need to supervise the tool at all.

## Key takeaways

- You can delegate work to AI, but never the responsibility for the result.
- Automation bias makes over-reliance easy — fluent output plus deadlines is the danger zone.
- Keep judgment in the loop for anything high-stakes, ambiguous, ethical, irreversible, or requiring sign-off.
- Treat outputs as proposals to evaluate, and keep your own expertise sharp.
